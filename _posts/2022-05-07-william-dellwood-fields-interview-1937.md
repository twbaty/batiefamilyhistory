<p>&nbsp;Biography Form</p>

<p>Works Progress Administration</p>
Indian-Pioneer History Project for Oklahoma 

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;">Interview with
William Dellwood Fields now living and settled in the Choctaw Nation.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes;">&nbsp; </span>He is seventy-eight years of age and lives in
Red Oak.<o:p></o:p></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;">Field Worker, Bradley
Bolinger; June 16, 1937</p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;"><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></p>

<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in; text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: large;">Early Indian Days and White Settler Days</span><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p><span style="font-size: 14pt; text-indent: 0.5in;">My father, Henderson Lewis Fields,
was born in Kentucky and was sixty-eight years of age when he died.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt; text-indent: 0.5in;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt; text-indent: 0.5in;">He was buried in Sebastian County, Aransas.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin-bottom: 0in;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p><span style="font-size: 14pt;">My mother’s name was Elizabeth Kilgore
Fields.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">My mother was buried in
Sebastian County, Arkansas.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">She was
taken back there for burial when she died at the age of eighty-nine.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I was raised until I was a big boy
right on the line of the Choctaw Nation and the state line of Arkansas.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I moved over into the Choctaw Nation along
about 1880.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I rented a place from a man
– a white man by the name of Tucker.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">He
was not an Indian citizen.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Mr. Tucker
had part of the land in cultivation more or less just in patches.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This place was located about six miles west
of where Poteau is located now.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I met my wife Sarah Jane Windham
Fields.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">We went back to Sebastian County,
Arkansas, and were married.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">We went back
to Arkansas because there were no white settlers in the nation in those days
who had authority to marry a white man and a white woman.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">We came back to the Choctaw Nation after our
wedding in Arkansas.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There were very few
white settlers in the Choctaw Nation when we settled in this country.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">We lived near what is now Poteau.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">We lived at this place about one year and
then moved to near where Red Oak is now located.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There was a Choctaw Indian Council
House and Court House near where Red Oak is now located.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There was a bunch of Choctaw Indians both
elder men and some of the young men of the Indian tribe.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I think as best I remember there were
fourteen in this bunch of Indians.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">They
were headed by a fullblood Choctaw by the name of Dixon Booth.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This Indian directed his bunch of Indian men
to go out in the nation and steal cattle and livestock. </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This country right where they were operating
was called the Sugar Loaf Country.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This
old Choctaw County line was located about three miles east of what is now Red
Oak; the other county to the west was named by the Choctaws, Gaines County.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The sheriff of what was the Sugar
Loaf County was a half breed Choctaw Indian.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The judge of Sugar Loaf County was a fullblood Choctaw Indian and was
reasonably well educated before he was sent to this new country from the South.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">His name was Benton, I do not remember his
given name.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The sheriff’s name was
Charley Wilson and he was also a half breed, half Choctaw and half white man.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This sheriff was called on to help
stop this gang of stealing in the nation; the thieves would gather up great
bunches of cattle and take them down to the near Poteau in the bottom country
of the Poteau River.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The Indians who owned quite a bit of
livestock got tired of this kind of treatment.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">In many instances a lot of these Indians who were interested in this
livestock stealing were arrested, but their leader, Dixon Booth, was able to
produce witnesses when any of his men were brought to the old Indian Court
House near Red Oak for trial.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">He would
manage to have witnesses there to testify that the charges were untrue and so
got the men dismissed.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This Indian sheriff by the name of
Charley Wilson was finally prevailed upon by the law abiding Choctaw of this
country to try again to stop the stealing.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This sheriff went to the Indian Judge, Mr. Benton, and told him that he
would go out and bring in these Indian men who were connected with this
livestock stealing if the judge would not let them get away again.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The old Indian Judge instructed Charley
Wilson to go out and bring these robbers in.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The sheriff of this part of Sugar Loaf County went out and brought in
fourteen men of the Choctaw Tribe.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There
were six middle aged Choctaw men and eight younger ones in this gang that was
brought in for trial before the old Indian court.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The old Indian Judge, Mr. Benton, found the
whole bunch guilty.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The old judge let
the eight younger Indians off as an example to the other young Choctaw Indians
who were growing up in this country.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The
old Indian judge sentenced the remaining six of the older Indian men who had
been taking part n the livestock stealing to be taken out at once and shot.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I went to this old Indian Court House
to listen to this great trial and the old Indian judge directed these six older
men to be executed at once.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I believe
there were in attendance at this trial and execution about two hundred or more
Indian men.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">They were all interested in
seeing this king of conduct stopped.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">After the trial was over the Indian
Judge turned to the eight younger men free with a long lecture in the Indian
language and directed the six older ones to be taken out and shot.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Every Indian sentenced to death had the
liberty to select one of his best friends to do the shooting.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Most of the time it is the duty of the Indian
sheriff to do the shooting himself but on this occasion the old Indian judge
allowed each of the six men to pick his friend to shoot him.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I was a very young man when this happened.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">These six prisoners were held there
in the Court House yard until the judge had directed a number of other Indian
men to construct six boxes to bury the prisoners in after they were shot.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">These six prisoners were stripped to the
waist of all their clothes and the boxes for their burial were put in a row in
front of this large red oak tree that stood in the court house yard.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The town of Red Oak is named today after this
famous tree.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">A mark was made over the
heart of each one of these six Indians after they were stripped to the
waist.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The sheriff directed the six men
who were going to do the shooting to back off to the front about ten steps.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">All six of the prisoners were then lined up,
sitting on the boxes in which they were to be buried, waiting for the Indian
sheriff to give the order for them to be shot.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">At the signal of the Indian sheriff all of the men picked to do the
shooting, fired.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I thought it seemed
just like all of them fired at the same time; it just sounded like one large
gun went off.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I listened to this
shooting and saw it done.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This did stop
a lot of the livestock stealing in the Nation close to where Red Oak is now
located.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">In my experience in the early day
there was a part for the Choctaw Tribe wo possessed real intelligence.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">These older Choctaws raised quite a few
livestock such as cattle and hogs and this class of Indians had better houses
to live in.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The other part of the tribe
just lived in small cabins on the creek back where there was always running
water.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">They did not try to dig wells in
this country in the early day.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Most of the furniture of these cabins
was just home made and a lot of their cooking utensils were hewed out of the
horns of wild deer and small cattle.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There were a few old style iron pots that they did some cooking in.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">They always had what they called stick and
mud fire places that the Indian women did the cooking over and in many
instances they just did some cooking out in the yard in the summer when the
weather was warm.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Most of the Indian
families had a gang of dogs that lived around them and around their
cabins.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">When an Indian woman was making
Tom Fuller or pa-shofa and one of the dogs came around where she was watching
the Tom Fuller cooking, she used a large wooden paddle to stir the Tom Fuller
with and if the dogs got too close to the cooking she would use the paddle that
she stirred with and gave the dog a good beating then would use the paddle back
in the cooking without even thinking of washing it.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">About all of the crops that were
raised by the Indian tribe were raised by the Indian women in what they called
Tom Fuller patches and a few sweet potatoes.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">They had great big pieces of logs hollowed out that they put this corn
in and beat it up with a small maul to remove the husk.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">My father tells me that the first and
only iron bridge that he could ever remember being built was across Fourshe
Maline Creek, about two miles east of where Wilburton is now located.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This was built by the soldiers who were
stationed at Fort Smith and was on the old Federal road that many of the
soldiers used in coming through this territory and was the only road that ran
through this country then.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">It was also
used by a sage coach company traveling through the territory on their way to
the Texas border.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I have been to this
spot and there are the remains of a member of old rusty iron beams lying
around.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">My father has told me that this
bridge was put there before the Civil War ever was started.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Jack McCurtain was the governor of
the Choctaw Tribe before his brother, Green McCurtain, was ever the
governor.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There were a few saw mills
just over in the state of Arkansas in those days.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I helped to haul the rough sawed lumber to
build this governor’s home.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">It was
located at the foot of a large mountain about one and one-half miles east of
the preset site of the town of Red Oak.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This was the Jack McCurtain home and he was then governor of the Choctaw
Tribe.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This was a large house, the
outside walls were made of hewed logs and sealed with this rough sawed
lumber.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This house had six
chimneys.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Mr. McCurtain had a number of
the younger Indian men to come and live there with him and to get all the wood
for these fire places in the winter time.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This Jack McCurtain and his brother were pretty intelligent and were
liked by the whole tribe.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">When I was a young man in the
Territory, one of my Indian neighbors was taken sick and I visited him.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I think his trouble and illness was called
pneumonia.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This Indian was lying on the
ground floor and like most of the ordinary Indians did not have any flooring in
his cabin.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">He had very little covering
and what covering he had was the sun-dried hides of animals.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">It seemed to me like he had a very hard bed
to be sick in.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I told him he was sick
and needed some kind of medical help.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">His wife said they had sent for an Indian doctor.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This old Indian doctor’s name was
Simon Peter.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The next day I was over to
see how the sick man was getting along.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This old Indian doctor had what I called a lot of superstitions.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">This old doctor had the sick Indian lying
face down and the doctor was up on top of him walking from head to foot on the
body of the sick man.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I was not able to
find out from the old doctor just what this treatment would do for the sick
Indian.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I visited this place again the
next day and the sick Indian was dead.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I
was unable to find out just what the family thought was his trouble.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">When I was a young man growing up in
this country when the Choctaws buried one of their family, they just fixed a
home made wooden box without any lining or anything and placed the body dressed
in the ordinary clothes worn by him every day and all his personal belonging
and all in the box with him and took the box out in the corner of the yard of
their cabin and buried it there.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">That
way they had of buying the dead was changed later and they had other burying
places.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The Choctaw Railroad came through
along about 1890, if I recall right; this railroad is now called the Rock
Island.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The Choctaw Railroad took the
place of the old stage road that ran from Fort Smith to the Texas border.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There was one place on the old road situated
on tope of a high mountain.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">It was
called Mountain Station by the stage line and everybody in this country then
knew of this place, which is still there.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">It is located about eight or nine miles south and west of Wilburton
now.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">The stages stopped at this place to
water their horses and all who were aboard.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;
</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">There was a stone house built over this great spring and part of the rocks
are still there and the spring is still running today.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">At one time when I was growing up in
this nation the Government sent a company of Federal soldiers into this Choctaw
Nation along about 1861, as I remember, to check and see if the white settlers
that had moved into the Nation had complied with the requirements of the
Choctaw Nation.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Some of the Choctaw
Indians in those days were not satisfied with some of the white settlers, but
this was worked out in a satisfactory way and all the white settlers filed
applications that entitled them to live in this Choctaw Nation.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">I was living in what is now Latimer County.</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%; margin-bottom: 0in;"><span style="font-size: 14.0pt; line-height: 150%;"><o:p>&nbsp;</o:p></span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">My father told me when I was a small
boy that he belonged to a regiment of mounted men of the Confederate Army.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">Part of these men were from the state of
Texas but there were many Choctaws in this regiment.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">He said that they scouted all over the Indian
country trying to rid the country of all Federal soldiers that came here.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp; </span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">He said this was in the year 1862.</span><span style="font-size: 14pt;">&nbsp;</span></p>
