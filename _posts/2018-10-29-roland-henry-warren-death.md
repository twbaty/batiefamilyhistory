"On February 2, 1942, the <i>Shark</i> reported to her base at 
Surabaya that she had been depth-charged ten miles off Tifore Island and
 had failed to sink a Japanese ship during a torpedo attack.  Five days 
later, she reported chasing an empty cargo ship headed northwest.  On 
February 8, the <i>Shark</i> was ordered to proceed to Makassar Strait via the north coast of Celebes.  Thereafter, the <i>Shark</i>
 was never heard from again.  On March 7, 1942, she was reported as 
presumed lost due to unknown causes.  The official announcement of the <i>Shark's</i> loss was made on March 18, 1942.&nbsp;<a href="http://www.subsowespac.org/world_war_ii_submarines/uss_shark_ss_174.shtml#note4">4</a>

<blockquote>
Navy Department Communiqué No. 57, March 18, 1942

The U. S. submarine <i>Shark</i> has been overdue in the Far East 
for more than a month and must be presumed to be lost.  The next of kin 
of the personnel of the <i>Shark</i> have been notified.

During the month of December, the U. S. submarine <i>Sealion</i> 
which was under extensive overhaul at Cavite, was so damaged as to 
necessitate her demolition to prevent her use by the enemy in the event 
of capture.</blockquote>

Japanese records reviewed after the war documented numerous attacks on unidentified submarines in the <i>Shark's</i>
 area at plausible times.  On February 11, 1942, the Japanese 
depth-charged a submarine east of Menado, northern Celebes.  On February
 17, they attacked an unidentified submarine off Kendari.  On February 
21, an enemy sub chaser rammed a U. S. submarine in Manipa Strait (this 
report could not be confirmed).  Based on the fact that on February 8 
the <i>Shark</i> had been sent to the area near Menado, she could have
 been the submarine the Japanese depth-charged.  Another report 
described an attack 120 miles east of Menado on February 11, 1942, at 
0137 hours, by the IJN destroyer <i>Yamakaze</i>, which sank a surfaced submarine with deck gun fire.&nbsp;<a href="http://www.subsowespac.org/world_war_ii_submarines/uss_shark_ss_174.shtml#note5">5</a>

The <i>Shark</i> was probably lost as a result of the attack by the <i>Yamakaze</i>, as described above, at the geographic position 
<a href="https://www.google.com/maps/place/1%C2%B045'00.0%22N+127%C2%B015'00.0%22E/@1.75,127.2478113,17z/data=!3m1!4b1!4m2!3m1!1s0x0:0x0?hl=en" target="_blank">
01° 45' N, 127° 15' E</a>.  Japanese records indicate the destroyer 
sighted a surfaced submarine and opened main battery fire at it, 
expending a total of forty-two five-inch shells and sixty rounds of 
machine gun ammunition during the action.  The submarine soon sank and 
voices were heard in the water, but no effort was made to locate and 
rescue survivors.  The other attacks discussed above are not considered 
to be feasible or related to the <i>Shark's</i> loss.  However, the evidence of her loss is not conclusive.  Therefore, the cause of the Shark's loss remains unknown.&nbsp;<a href="http://www.subsowespac.org/world_war_ii_submarines/uss_shark_ss_174.shtml#note6">6</a>"

Rest in Peace Roland....
