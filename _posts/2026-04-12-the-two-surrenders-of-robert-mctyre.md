<p data-path-to-node="4">Most soldiers saw the end of the Civil War once. My 3rd great-grandfather, <b data-index-in-node="75" data-path-to-node="4">Robert W. McTyre</b>, saw it twice.</p><p data-path-to-node="5">Robert was a young man of 23 when he walked into Marietta, Georgia, in March 1862. He signed his name, took a <b data-index-in-node="110" data-path-to-node="5">$50 enlistment bounty</b>—likely a small fortune to send home to his wife, Amanda, and their infant son—and joined the 41st Georgia Infantry. He didn't know then that he was beginning a three-year odyssey that would take him through the most iconic and devastating moments of the war.</p><h3 data-path-to-node="6">The First End: The Heat of Vicksburg</h3><p data-path-to-node="7">By July 1863, the romance of the war was gone. Robert was trapped in the <b data-index-in-node="73" data-path-to-node="7">Siege of Vicksburg</b>, a 47-day nightmare of constant shelling and starvation. On July 4th, 1863, while the North celebrated, Robert became a Prisoner of War.</p><p data-path-to-node="8">I found his <b data-index-in-node="12" data-path-to-node="8">Parole</b> from that day. It was a "gentleman’s agreement"—the Union let him walk away, provided he promised never to pick up a gun again until he was officially "exchanged." For most men, that would have been the end of their war.</p><p data-path-to-node="8"></p><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEi1j6cV7u7I20We3kZhaYyfgXHP6mtKpuxi99JO-7wly6l7QJAXYz3MkbgRUBNUoEGGM3lXlzWFhfxipj1iCE7uQyCAIxOHPIMw2PDS6rCUf1OyIh6itdrlIM44iHlv5jUSnFKWq-BrECCtHtrvG34RlrkqYpEpH3igTnuDxAZoS58jAuDujRqCQTsic-IZ/s1600/Page%2012.jpg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="1233" data-original-width="1600" height="247" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEi1j6cV7u7I20We3kZhaYyfgXHP6mtKpuxi99JO-7wly6l7QJAXYz3MkbgRUBNUoEGGM3lXlzWFhfxipj1iCE7uQyCAIxOHPIMw2PDS6rCUf1OyIh6itdrlIM44iHlv5jUSnFKWq-BrECCtHtrvG34RlrkqYpEpH3igTnuDxAZoS58jAuDujRqCQTsic-IZ/s320/Page%2012.jpg" width="320" /></a></div><br />&nbsp;<p></p><h3 data-path-to-node="9">The Second End: The Remnant of the 40th</h3><p data-path-to-node="10">But Robert’s story didn't stop in Mississippi. Military records from 1915 reveal a shocking twist: Robert was eventually "exchanged" and went right back to the front.</p><p data-path-to-node="11">By 1865, his original regiment had been practically wiped out. The few survivors left were consolidated into the <b data-index-in-node="113" data-path-to-node="11">40th Battalion Georgia Infantry</b>. They were the "remnant"—the men who refused to quit even as the Confederacy collapsed around them.</p><p data-path-to-node="12">On <b data-index-in-node="3" data-path-to-node="12">May 1, 1865</b>, nearly two years after Vicksburg, Robert stood in <b data-index-in-node="66" data-path-to-node="12">Greensboro, North Carolina</b>. He watched as General Joseph E. Johnston surrendered the last major Confederate army to General William T. Sherman. There, Robert signed his <i data-index-in-node="235" data-path-to-node="12">second</i> set of surrender papers. He had fought from the Mississippi River to the Atlantic coast, surviving to the very last hour.</p><h3 data-path-to-node="13">A New Life in the West</h3><p data-path-to-node="14">The resilience Robert showed on the battlefield defined his life as a civilian. He returned to Georgia, worked as a <b data-index-in-node="116" data-path-to-node="14">Railroad Foreman</b>—literally rebuilding the tracks he had once defended—and eventually led his wife and nine children on a new mission: settling the frontier.</p><p data-path-to-node="15">By 1880, the McTyres had moved to <b data-index-in-node="34" data-path-to-node="15">Franklin, Arkansas</b>, and eventually, the family legacy stretched into <b data-index-in-node="103" data-path-to-node="15">Oklahoma</b> and <b data-index-in-node="116" data-path-to-node="15">Texas</b>. We know this because, in 1915, the Oklahoma Pension Board had to verify his service records. They found that Private McTyre hadn't just "joined the war"; he had seen it through to the bitter, final end.</p><p data-path-to-node="15"></p><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEh5JOhh_qgMTBIqk3LUIz2yHjeIaFGIoogw11kNGz9yDfJPWsC7Mt7dlpVzfupkgGeTmiYFzt1M9QZlFkAaGGJyvP33-PL4tWHyIHBduAtSLAuaXx5bsLeb52hS1PlYjPBg7ZJiHtz7aiRQNyNG8vS5kgYUVFn6_RAkNCXehslXRHidylexBg4poScSVLPt/s1600/Page%202(1).jpg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="1600" data-original-width="694" height="320" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEh5JOhh_qgMTBIqk3LUIz2yHjeIaFGIoogw11kNGz9yDfJPWsC7Mt7dlpVzfupkgGeTmiYFzt1M9QZlFkAaGGJyvP33-PL4tWHyIHBduAtSLAuaXx5bsLeb52hS1PlYjPBg7ZJiHtz7aiRQNyNG8vS5kgYUVFn6_RAkNCXehslXRHidylexBg4poScSVLPt/s320/Page%202(1).jpg" width="139" /></a></div><br /><p></p><p data-path-to-node="16">Robert W. McTyre began as a day laborer in Georgia and ended as a pioneer in the West. He was a man of his word, a man of many oaths, and a survivor who helped build the world we live in today.</p><p data-path-to-node="16"></p><div style="text-align: center;">~ Tom ~</div><div style="text-align: center;"><div>CADECBAB</div><div>11 April 2026</div></div><p data-path-to-node="16">&nbsp;</p><br />
